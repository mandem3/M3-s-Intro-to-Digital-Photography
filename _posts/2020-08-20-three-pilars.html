Three Pillars of Photography #3 (Shutter Speed)

We've discussed ISO and Aperture, so that leaves the third pillar of photography: Shutter Speed.  Your last micro-experiment for the week is included in this announcement. The "Pillars of Photography" micro-experiments are not graded assignments, but I strongly encourage you to do them.

ABOUT  SHUTTER  SPEED

.
In both film and digital cameras, the shutter speed determines the length of exposure (the amount of time during which light enters the camera and hits the film or digital sensor).  In film cameras and older digital cameras, there was a moving part inside the camera body that slid in front of the film or sensor to manually block the light.  In some newer cameras, the digital sensor just stops recording instead. 
.
Shutter speed is measured in seconds. Most photographs are exposed for a fraction of a second. 
.
Shutter speed has two effects.  The most obvious one is that a longer shutter speed lets more light in and the resulting image is brighter, while a shorter shutter speed means less light and a darker picture.  However, remember that light is not passive input — it reflects a constantly-moving world. If you capture the light coming from 1/4000th of a second, then you'll get a reflection of very short slice of movement.  Assuming you've set your focus correctly, the image will be very crisp and seem to "stop time."  Likewise, as you capture the light from more extended periods, the camera will pick up the movement of the world outside its box.  A moderately long exposure such as 1/6 will capture a motion blur on sudden movements (and probably camera shake). With a tripod and a 3 second shutter speed, you can capture a world of ghostly pedestrians. With an extremely long exposure, you can capture the movement of the earth itself (i.e., star trails)

If you want more information on shutter speeds, this article is awesome.  The simple takeaway is:  

 
.
Longer shutter speed = More light + More motion blur.

.

Shutter Speed Experiment: Getting to Know Your Camera


For this experiment, you'll need an assistant. If you have a kid old enough to understand the task, they make great assistants and it's a good family activity.  Otherwise, you can use a spouse, friend, co-worker, pet — or even a self-moving object, like an oscillating fan. If you have a tripod, you'll want to use it for this assignment. Otherwise, set the camera down on an unmoving surface (e.g., a pile of books on a table) and try to avoid touching it very much. If you have a remote control for your camera, you may want to use it to further prevent shaking the camera.

.

  1. Have your assistant sit in one spot and make sure they're in focus.  

  2. Find your shutter speed controls. (You may need to reference your camera's owner manual.)

  3. Set your ISO to the lowest setting and then use your camera's automatic settings to find your "average" aperture for this project. Then, change your shutter speed to 3 seconds. Have your assistant wiggle a lot while you take a picture. (This should end up very bright.)

  4. Next, use aperture, ISO, and ambient light to make the background image properly exposed, and try again. Have your assistant wiggle a lot while you take a picture. 

  5. Set your ISO to the lowest setting (again) and also set aperture to the smallest f-stop. Take photos of your wiggly assistant until you find out what shutter speed you need to balance out that setting. 

  6. Without changing ISO, change your aperture to its largest f-stop.  Take photos of your wiggly assistant until you find out what shutter speed you need to balance out that setting. 

  7. Set your ISO no higher than 500.  Use your aperture and shutter speed together to try to freeze your wiggly assistant in mid-motion without any blur in the image. Try not to underexpose the image (you may not be able to achieve this, depending on the lighting). 

  8. Consider what you observed. Were you able to freeze motion?  Were you able to create interesting blurs?
